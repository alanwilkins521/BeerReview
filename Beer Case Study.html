Procedure

This study is the capstone project for the Google Data Analytics career certificate. As such, I will be following the Google data analysis 6 step process of ask, prepare, process, analyze, share, and act. These follow the full data analysis from start to finish.

1. Ask

To start this study, we must create questions that a potential stakeholder might ask of a data analyst to gain business insights. Since I currently work at a brewery, Ive chosen to look at data on beer reviews. Ive chosen to simulate the work of a data analyst being asked these questions by the owner of a small local brewery:

  • We already have a solid Pale Ale and IPA. What are some lighter or darker style beers that we should think about producing?
  • Which styles optimize flavor, aroma, and appearance?
  • Which are the most popular breweries that we should visit for research and development?

These are all very realistic questions that a brewery owner would be interested in, and can be answered with the right data. I have found a beer review dataset on kaggle.com, linked here, which I will be using for this analysis.

To do this, we will complete a quick ROCCC analysis of our dataset, asking if it is reliable, original, current, comprehensive, and cited.   • Comprehensive: With over 1.5 million entries recorded in this dataset, I would say the sample size is more than enough to deem it comprehensive.
  • Cited: This work has been downloaded almost 6000 times on Kaggle, and used in 13 others notebooks. There isnt much other information on citations of this set, but again, BeerAdvocate is a very well known and cited website for beer reviews and information.

3. Process

Since we have such a large dataset, I moved the data into Googles cloud services to query and clean our data in SQL. First, looking at the data detail section of the Kaggle website, we can see that we will need to aggregate our data for each beer, because there are multiple reviews for each one. We will use SQL to find the average rating for each category for every beer that is listed.

Second, we will want to know how many reviews each of the beers have, so that we only look at beers with a significant number of reviews. I have set that as a minimum of 100 reviews.

I will also sort our filtered and aggregated data by overall score, and use SELECT DISTINCT to make sure there are no duplicate entries in our analysis. From there, we can manipulate and analyze the data in R. In full, the SQL query that I ran looked like this:

SELECT DISTINCT
  COUNT(beer_name) AS number_reviews,
  brewery_name,
  beer_style,
  beer_name,
  AVG(review_overall) AS overall,
  AVG(review_aroma) AS aroma,
  AVG(review_appearance) AS appearance,
  AVG(review_palate) AS mouthfeel,
  AVG(review_taste) AS taste
FROM
  `beerdataproject.Beer_Reviews.Beer_Reviews_Raw`
GROUP BY
  beer_name,
  brewery_name,
  beer_style
HAVING COUNT(beer_name) >= 100
ORDER BY
  overall DESC

The resulting data set includes just 2,759 beers that we can analyze and help use to answer our business questions. We will now import this new csv into R Studio to analyze:

6. Act

Finally, after our work on the data and subsequent analysis, we come to conclusions in the form of recommendations to the brewery to help them answer their business questions from before. Those questions, along with our answers, are here:

  • We already have a solid Pale Ale and IPA. What are some lighter or darker style beers that we should think about producing?

  • Which styles optimize flavor, aroma, and appearance?

    • If you were to pick one, we would suggest brewing a darker style beer first, as they get the most reviews after the Pale Ale/IPA category.

    • Of the dark styles, Russian Imperial Stouts, American Porters, and American Amber Ales get the most reviews.

    • Of those, Russian Imperial Stouts and American Porters get the most quality reviews, being in the top quartile of aroma, flavor, and appearance.

    • As for the lighter styles, the highly reviewed styles are Saison/Farmhouse Ales, Fruit Beers, and American Adjunct Lagers

    • Out of those, the most quality examples come from Saison/Farmhouse Ales

    • In conclusion, if there were one dark style and one light style I would recommend brewing next, they would be a Russian Imperial Stout, and a Saison/Farmhouse Ale, respectively.

  • Which are the most popular breweries that we should visit for research and development?

    • The top 4 breweries in the category of most beers with 100+ reviews on BeerAdvocate are Rogue Ales, Sam Adams, Matt Brewing, and New Glarus.

    • The one with the most beers in that category is Sam Adams, while Rogue and New Glarus have more quality examples.

In conclusion, the Beer Advocate reviews data, while not perfect, gave us 1.5 million observations to pull insights from, and we were able to answer all of the hypothetical stakeholder questions quite easily. Our suggested actions are to try brewing two new styles of beer, and to visit the top 4 reviewed breweries that we listed.

On top of that, because our data integrity was sub optimal, I would suggest that the business continue the research done here by creating a survey of their own local customers and market, to get a better idea of what their specific customers needs are.
